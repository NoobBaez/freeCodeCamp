Nicky Winton

  • 1909 - Born in Hampstead, London.

  • 1923 - Entered Stowe School. He left without qualifications.

  • 1931 - Moved to France and worked for the Banque Nationale de Crédit in Paris.

  • 1938 - At school, he had become an outstanding fencer and was selected for the British team.

  • 1938 - A call for help from Marie Schmolka and Doreen Warriner, he decided to visit Prague and help Martin Blake, who was in Prague as an associate of the British Committee for Refugees from Czechoslovakia

  • 1939 - Found homes in Britain for 669 children, many of whose parents would perish in the Auschwitz concentration camp.

  • 1948 - Married Grete Gjelstrup, a Danish secretary.

  • 1988 - His wife Grete found a scrapbook in the attic of their home. The document contained a list of the evacuated children, including their parents’ names and the names and addresses of the foster families.

  • 1909 - During an episode of the BBC television programme That's Life! when he was invited as a member of the audience. At one point, Winton's scrapbook was shown and his achievements were explained. The host of the programme, Esther Rantzen, asked whether anybody in the audience owed their lives to Winton, and if so, to stand: more than two dozen people surrounding Winton rose and applauded. Ms Rantzen then asked if anyone present was the child or grandchild of one of the children Winton saved. The rest of the audience stood.

  • 2015 - Awarded the Freedom of the City of London

  • 2015 - Died in his sleep at Wexham Park Hospital in Slough

  • 2020 - Google honoured Winton's legacy and celebrated what would have been his 111th birthday with a doodle.

“If something isn’t blatantly impossible, then there must be a way of doing it.” Nicky Winton.
